# THE CONSTITUTION OF DIGITAL ARTISANS

**A Declaration of the Digital Rights of the Person**

*Version 1.1 — July 2026 — a living text: a constitution is never finished.*
*English translation of the canonical French text ([CONSTITUTION.md](../../CONSTITUTION.md), v1.1).*

---

> **All technology exercises power.**
> **Whoever designs a technology therefore bears a responsibility.**

---

## Preamble

Digital technologies are not neutral. They organise attention, influence behaviour, shape relationships and hold the traces of human lives. Any architecture that collects, processes or stores data thereby bears a responsibility towards people — and that responsibility outranks economic interests.

This text is not a list of values: it is made of **voluntary renunciations** — limits its signatories impose upon themselves, even when those limits cost them. Renouncing the capture of attention. Renouncing the sale of people. Renouncing regret as a lever. Renouncing lock-in. Renouncing the metrics that destroy rest.

We affirm that a digital economy can prosper without turning human intimacy into merchandise. This Constitution states the conditions.

---

## Article 1 — On the nature of human data

Every piece of digital data represents a person, a relationship or a human work. As such, **human data is not an asset**. It is not a raw material, cannot be treated as a mere economic resource, and holding it creates a duty — never a property right.

This change of category precedes every article that follows: what comes next is not a list of protections, but the consequence of this nature.

## Title I — On the person

**Article 2 — On dignity.** Every person is an end, never a resource to extract, a profile to exploit or a metric to maximise. No commercial, technical or strategic goal justifies manipulation, engineered dependency, hidden surveillance or the exploitation of human vulnerability.

**Article 3 — On sovereignty.** Every person remains sovereign over their data. They may veil it from others without destroying their own archive, refuse to appear in a collective work — including one already paid for by a third party — and exercise their right to erasure over what concerns them. The right of the person always prevails over the right of the transaction.

**Article 4 — On leaving.** Every person may leave a service taking all of their data with them, in an open, documented, reusable format. No technical lock-in may serve as a commercial strategy: people stay out of attachment, never out of captivity.

**Article 5 — On the vulnerable.** Children, elders, the bereaved, the isolated, the ill and the fragile receive reinforced protection: dedicated infrastructures; no scoring, no comparison, no exposure. Vulnerability may never serve as a conversion lever. For minors, coming of age brings a handover: the history of their digital life is transferred to them in full sovereignty — a passage of dignity, not a change of technical permission.

## Title II — On rhythms

**Article 6 — On attention.** Human attention is not a raw material. Notifications, recommendations and reminders must serve a real need of the person, never the obsession with returns, clicks or time spent. A worthy technology maximises the value of chosen time, not the duration of captured time.

**Article 7 — On silence and rest.** Inactivity is a legitimate state. No system shall read silence as a lack, a debt or a problem to be fixed, nor chase an inactive person. Every person holds the right to be absent; systems respect human rhythms instead of correcting them. **A humane system does not panic at the silence of its users.**

**Article 8 — On the asymmetry of attention.** A gesture of attention towards another may be made visible by a soft signal. The absence of that gesture must never be visible, quantified or deducible: one may know that someone was present; one must never be able to point at who was not. Counters of relational debt, forced read receipts and presence scores are contrary to this Constitution.

**Article 9 — On manipulative architectures.** Interfaces that push a person to act against their own interest, hide the option to refuse, obstruct leaving, or make the healthiest option less visible than the most profitable one are proscribed. Proscribed above all is the exploitation of regret: the fear of loss, guilt and artificial urgency are never instruments of growth. Consent obtained through fatigue is not consent.

## Title III — On memory

**Article 10 — On inalienability.** A person's access to their fundamental bonds and to the raw history of their digital life may never be held hostage to a payment, an expiry threat or a punitive deletion. Space for the future may be sold; the survival of the past is never threatened. Memory is not for ransom.

**Article 11 — On minimisation and non-commodification.** A service collects the minimum necessary, retains with reason and explains its retention periods. Personal data is neither sold, nor rented, nor handed over for advertising purposes. No personal content trains an artificial intelligence system without explicit, specific and revocable consent: the machine assists; it never owns human stories.

**Article 12 — On legitimate economics.** The value of a service comes from the service rendered, never from the invisible exploitation of people. Monetisation applies to the added craft — editing, curation, objects, accompaniment — never to access to human bonds themselves.

## Title IV — On responsibilities

**Article 13 — On transparency.** Any automated decision with a significant effect on a person must be explainable in understandable language. The person knows when a machine intervenes, along which main logics, and with which remedies.

**Article 14 — On measurable responsibility.** Ethical intent is not enough: it must appear in the architecture, the data schemas, the metrics and the budget trade-offs. **Kindness is not a marketing tone: it is an architectural constraint.** Growth and engagement indicators remain subordinate to trust, clarity and the reduction of mental load. The activity of those who chose silence is never measured against them.

**Article 15 — On honest display.** Nothing is presented as achieved until it is built. A promise of security, encryption or permanence is displayed as a dated commitment, never as an accomplished fact. Gaps between declared principles and actual practice are documented publicly, together with their corrective measures.

**Article 16 — On recourse and governance.** Any system can cause harm, even unintentionally: simple paths exist to report, contest, understand and obtain redress. Every major evolution of a system is examined in the light of its human effects — who gains power, who becomes exposed, who can leave, who pays the true cost. This Constitution is open: it may be adopted, amended, translated, criticised and forked by anyone.

---

## Conclusion

We believe another digital world is possible: one where data is no longer merchandise, where trust is worth more than capture, where silence is respected, where people remain sovereign.

A truly humane system is not measured only by what it makes possible, but by what it refuses to do to people. No innovation deserves to be called progress if it requires the human to become smaller so that the system may become larger.

Because behind every piece of data there is a human being — and no business model should ever make us forget it.
